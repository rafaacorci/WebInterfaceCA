Fixed Gear Bikes: A Ride Back to Simplicity

Fixed gear bikes—often lovingly called fixies—are a minimalist’s dream on two wheels. Unlike most modern bicycles, fixed gear bikes have no freewheel mechanism. That means when the wheels turn, so do the pedals—no coasting, just pure connection between rider and machine.

A Brief History

Fixed gear bikes trace their roots back to the early days of cycling in the 19th century. Before derailleur gears and multi-speed cassettes became the norm, all bikes were essentially fixed gear. They were reliable, simple, and easy to maintain—qualities that have led to their modern-day resurgence.

Track cycling brought fixed gear bikes into the competitive scene, with velodrome racers embracing the direct drive for its efficiency and responsiveness. Over time, urban cyclists, bike messengers, and minimalist riders began adopting fixies for their clean aesthetics, low maintenance needs, and the raw, responsive riding experience they offer.

Why Ride Fixed?

Riding a fixed gear bike is all about control, rhythm, and feel. With fewer components, there's less weight and less that can go wrong. Many riders say that riding fixed improves their cadence, handling, and connection to the road. Plus, in busy cities, the simplicity of a fixie means more time riding and less time wrenching.

Whether you're drawn to the elegant lines, the tight feedback from the drivetrain, or the throwback to cycling's golden age, fixed gear bikes offer a unique experience that’s hard to replicate.
